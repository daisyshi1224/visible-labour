Visible Labour: Everyday Work in Urban Space

London, Ontario, 1900s–1920s

This exhibition explores how everyday work shaped urban life in London, Ontario between the 1900s and 1920s. Drawing on archival photographs from Western Archives (Access to Memory), it examines factories, workshops, commercial interiors, and urban infrastructure as spaces where labour, technology, and social organization intersect. Rather than treating photographs as simple documentation, this project foregrounds how work becomes visible through space. Machines, counters, tools, and bodies are arranged to structure production and interaction. Across six thematic sections, the images reveal how labour is organized and how industrial modernity reshaped the rhythms of everyday urban life.

Work in Public: Small Businesses and Retail Interiors

Shops and small businesses made labour visible in public. Counters, displays, and decorated interiors show how commerce blended workplace and social space, often family-run, seasonal, and tied to neighbourhood routines.

Work as Collective Identity: The Industrial Workforce

Factories and workplaces present labour as a shared activity. Group formations and organized environments show how work is structured collectively within industrial systems.

Work with Machines: Factory Interiors and Mechanized Systems

Factory interiors place workers within machine-centered environments. Equipment, production lines, and spatial layouts show how labour is organized for efficiency and output.

Work as Skill: Craft, Repair, and Material Transformation

Workshops and repair spaces highlight skilled manual labour. Tools, materials, and work environments show how labour is shaped through hands-on processes and experience.

Women at Work: Gender and Visibility

These spaces show women working within factories and commercial environments. Organized interiors and production systems reveal how labour is structured through gendered roles and visibility.

Work in Urban Infrastructure: Building and Maintaining the City

Urban spaces show labour involved in building and maintaining the city. Open environments and construction sites reveal how work directly shapes the physical landscape.
